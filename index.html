Abstract

Understanding floral morphology is central to plant evolutionary biology, yet capturing the full three-dimensional geometry of flowers at scale remains a long-standing challenge. Traditional morphometric approaches rely on manually collected landmarks or two-dimensional measurements that inevitably discard depth information and are labour-intensive to obtain.

This project develops an end-to-end machine-learning pipeline that reconstructs dense 3D flower models from ordinary photographs and automatically extracts biologically meaningful shape descriptors. The reconstructed shapes are then embedded in a phylogenetically informed latent space to study how floral form has diversified across flowering-plant lineages.

Our approach combines neural implicit surfaces, differentiable rendering, and geometric deep learning to make large-scale 3D phenotyping feasible from image collections that already exist in herbarium and biodiversity databases.

Pipeline Overview

The pipeline consists of four tightly integrated stages, from raw image input to downstream evolutionary analysis.

📷

Image Collection

Multi-view or single-view photographs of flowers sourced from herbarium scans and field photography.

🤖

3D Reconstruction

Neural radiance fields and implicit-surface networks reconstruct dense, watertight meshes per specimen.

📈

Shape Encoding

Geometric deep learning extracts rotation-invariant descriptors that capture petal curvature, symmetry, and spatial arrangement.

🌿

Evolutionary Analysis

Descriptors are projected onto a phylogenetic tree to quantify convergence, divergence, and rates of shape change.
